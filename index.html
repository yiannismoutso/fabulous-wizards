IT technologies

Cyber security is the progress of protecting networks, systems, programs, and data from cyber-attacks. These attacks are aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information of a company. A successful cyber security approach has layers of protection on the computers, networks, programs, or data that people intend to keep safe. They also can be done to extort money. An effective defence against a cyber-attack requires the cooperation of many components to be successful. This means that a cyber security system doesn’t only rely on cyber defence technology but also relies on people making smart cyber defence choices. Organizations with a fine cybersecurity strategy uses the tools of advanced analytics, artificial intelligence, and machine learning, which helps fight cyberthreats more effectively and impacts breaches when they occur. A strong cybersecurity strategy has layers of protections to defend against cybercrime. The protections are as follows: Critical infrastructure security: practises for protecting the computer systems, networks, and other assets that rely on up national security, economic health and public safety. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has created a cybersecurity framework to help organizations in this area, while the U.S department of Homeland Security provides additional guidance.

Network Security:

protecting a computer network from intruders, including both wired and wireless connections.

Application security:

help protect applications operating on premises and in the cloud. Security should be built into applications at the design stage, with considerations for how data is handled, user authentications, etc.

Cloud security:

computing that encrypts cloud data in storage and as it travels to, from and within the cloud, to support customer privacy, business requirements and regulatory compliance standards.

Information security:

data protection measures such as the General Data Protection Regulation that secure the most sensitive data from theft, exposure, and unauthorized access.

End- user education:

building security awareness across the organization to strengthen endpoint security. For example, users can be trained to delete suspicious email attachments, avoid using unknown USB devices.

Disaster recovery:

tools and procedures for responding to unplanned events such as natural disasters, power outages, or cyberattacks, with minimal to no disruption to key operations.

Storage security:

includes encryption, immutable and isolated data copies. These remain in the same pool so they can quickly be restored to support recovery, minimizing the impact of a cyber-attack.

Mobile security:

enables you to manage and secure your mobile workforce with app security, container app security and secure mobile email.

Key cybersecurity technologies and best practises

Identity and access management (IAM)

:

this defines the roles for each user and their conditions which they are granted for their privileges. Methodologies include sign-on, which enables the user to log in to a network once without re-entering their credentials again during the same session. Multifactor authentication makes it safer for the user because it requires two or more access credentials. Privileged user accounts grant privileges to certain users only. IAM tools can also provide deeper visibility to cybersecurity professionals, including end points they can’t physically access.

Comprehensive data security platform

protects sensitive information across multi cloud environments. It also has ongoing monitoring that alerts them to data vulnerabilities and risks before they become data breaches. Backups and encryption are vital for keeping data safe.

Security information and event management (SIEM):

analyses data from security events to automatically delete suspicious user activities and trigger an immediate response. SIEM solutions include advanced detection methods such as behaviour analytics and artificial intelligence. They can automatically prioritize cyber threat response in line with your organization’s risk management objectives.

These technologies better cyber security by reducing the vulnerability to cyber-attacks and protecting critical information systems, without making user or customer service worse. An autonomous vehicle

An autonomous vehicle can drive by itself from a point A to B. It’s capable of sensing its environment and can work without human involvement. There are different levels to driving automation. Level 0, which is fully manual and level 5, which is fully autonomous. Level 0 to level 2 is where the driving environment is mostly controlled by a human, but the car has features like performing steering like lane assist or accelerating/ staying at the same speed as cruise control. Level 3 to level 5 is more automated as the system monitors the driving environment without the human having to get involved. The person can still override and act if it’s necessary, but they don’t have to. These vehicles rely on sensors, algorithms, machine learning systems and powerful processors to execute software. With these features, they create a map of their surroundings based on the sensors located on different parts of the vehicle. Video cameras detect traffic lights, read road signs, track other vehicles, and look for pedestrians. Ultrasonic sensors in the wheels detect curbs other vehicles to make it able for the vehicle to park. A sophisticated software then processes all these sensors and sends signals to the car’s actuators which then controls acceleration, braking and steering. Hard-coded rules, obstacle avoidance algorithms, predictive modelling, and object recognition help the software follow traffic rules and navigate obstacles. Obviously, fully autonomous cars are still undergoing testing. None are available to the public just yet. There are a few challenges ranging from technological to environmental and philosophical. Lidar (light detection) is expensive and is still improving to find the right balance between range and resolution. Will the cameras and sensors be able to track them? Will they have problems in tunnels and bridges? What happens on the event of an accident, who would be liable for it? the passenger? The manufacturer? The level 5 fully autonomous vehicles don’t have a dashboard nor a steering wheel, so a human passenger won’t have the option to take control in an emergency.

Robotics

is the science and engineering that produces machines, called robots that replicate human actions. They consist of mechanical arms. They are programmable machines that are built to assist humans. They were originally built to handle monotonous tasks, but they have expanded their abilities to perform tasks like cleaning homes, fighting fires and even perform surgeries. Each robot has different abilities. Some are controlled by humans and perform tasks that a human has full control of, and others are fully autonomous that perform tasks without a human influence.

As technology progresses, so do robotics. In 2005, robots were mainly found assembling cars in automotive factories. They consist of mechanical arms that are tasked to weld or screw certain parts of a car. Today, robots are that expanded, that assist in earth’s harshest conditions and even in healthcare.

There are a few types of robots. They come in different shapes and sizes depending on the task they need to do. They vary in design, functionality and autonomy. Robots these days are design to carry out tasks that humans can’t do. Normally, there are 5 types of robots:

Pre-programmed robots operate in an environment where they do simple tasks. An example of a robot like that would be a mechanical arm on an automotive assembly line. The arm welds the door, to then insert a part into the engine. Its task is to perform this job for longer, faster and more efficiently than a human.

Humanoid robots mimic human behaviour. They perform activities like walking, running, jumping and carrying objects. Some of them even look like us, by having human faces and expressions.

Autonomous robots operate in open environments that do not require human influence. They use sensors to perceive the world around them, and then apply decision-making structures to take the next step depending on the task they are performing. An example of an autonomous robot would be the Roomba vacuum cleaner, senses the house to roam freely throughout a home.

Teleoperated robots are semi-autonomous bots that use a wireless network to enable human control from a distance. They usually work in extreme geographical conditions. Examples of teleoperated robots are human-controlled submarines used to fix underwater pipe leaks.

Augmenting robots enhance capabilities that a human may have lost. The field of robotics for human augmentation is to redefine the definition of humanity by making humans faster and stronger. Some examples of current augmenting robots are exoskeletons used to lift very heavy weights.

The way robots’ function is that they are capable of being completely autonomous and independent from humans. They require more intense programming but allow robots to do tasks where the environment is not appropriate for humans. However, they are the most disruptive to society, eliminating low-wage jobs but at the same tie presenting new possibilities for growth. Dependent robots are non-autonomous robots that interact with humans to improve their abilities. A famous example of a dependent robot was created for a man whose arm was amputated. He was fitted with an MPL as a test so researchers could study its use for potential improvements and risks. The MPL was controlled by via electromyography and signals being sent from his amputated graph that controls the prosthesis. Over time, he became more efficient in controlling it and the signals sent became smaller and less variable, leading to more accuracy in its movements and allowing him to perform more complicated tasks.

Robots are built to present solutions to various needs and fulfills several different purposes. Therefore, they require a variety of components that to complete these tasks. However, there are several components that are necessary to the robot’s construction, like a power source. Computation includes all the components that make up a robot’s control system. The control systems are programmed to tell a robot how to utilize its components, similar to how the human brain sends signals to the body. Sensors provide a robot with stimuli in the form of electrical signals. This helps the robot sense what’s around it so it can interact with the outside world. These include video cameras that function as eyes, photoresistors that react to light and microphones that operate like ears. Actuators can be made of a variety of materials, such as metal and elastic. They are commonly operated by air (pneumatic operators) or oil (hydraulic actuators). A robot device can be considered a robot only if it has a movable frame. Actuators are responsible for the movement. The components are made up of motors that receive signals from the control system and move to carry out the movement necessary to complete a task.

In some industries, robots are replacing human jobs, which can create economic problems. After all, robots can only do what they are told to do, meaning they can’t expand their abilities. Robots cannot compete with human’s eyesight. Robots can’t compete with a human’s ability to understand what they see. The robots’ initial cost would be very expensive. Maintenance and the need for extra components would also be very expensive.

It's unlikely for AI’s to take over the world as the robots’ brain cannot compete with the one of the humans. Business network PwC predicts that up to 30% of jobs could be automated by robots by the mid-2030s. Other reports predict that the stock of robots could reach 20 million by 2030, with automated workers taking up 51 million jobs in the next 10 years. While they may not complete take over the world, we can expect to see more robots in our daily lives.
